Why do We Exist?

The Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) under the authority of the Office of Business Management (OMB) has begun an effort to address the security problems introduced by the recent increase of cyber-attacks on small and independent practices (Doctor Offices). In 2015, over 146,000,000 data records were lost by independent practices with the potential penalty of up to $210.00 per record. Congress has consecutively reduced the budget of the OMB year over year. With the HHS budget depending on the OMBs budget the HHS has issued new guidance to increase department revenue by focusing on independent practices. The large potential for revenue through penalties will cause an increase of “perceived” breaches with the small and independent practices through this re-focusing effort.

Confidentiality Agreements/

Having the proper agreements and contracts will serve as a foundation for having a successful security program. This can be invaluable should you or your partners have a data breach. Contracts will serve as a bases for determining ownership of the ePHI data and can keep you off the HIPAA Wall of Shame

Physical Access Control/

Independent practices are often found to be the victims of opportunistic robberies. Monitoring services often overlook your PHI data which can result in a breach.

System Security/

Independent practices have reported difficulties protecting their systems from Malware. The best known method to protect your computer systems is to base the controls and tools on known and well received industry standards.

Network Security/

Network security offers the greatest value for protecting most independent practices. Common malware requires network access and will connect to countries like China and Russia. Simple network controls can go a long way in preventing risk.

/Vulnerability Scanning

Software is often released with bugs and vulnerabilities. Vulnerability scanning is a process in which third-party companies compile a list of known vulnerabilities that provides a way of finding them. This allows us to patch or block the vulnerabilities further protecting the information system.

/Security Awareness

Attackers often use a process called social engineering to gain unauthorized access into an information system by tricking users into giving out important information. This method is so common security training is required by the HIPAA safeguards.

/Personnel Screening

Independent practices are often unable to absorb the risks and liability that may come from bad hiring decisions. On the one hand, employers are being sued by victims that allege the employer failed to exercise due diligence in hiring. Conversely, employers also face lawsuits from job applicants complaining about the accuracy of background reports or the failure to meet regulatory guidelines.

/Security Program Goverance

The HIPAA Security Rule requires covered entities to maintain reasonable and appropriate administrative, technical, and physical safeguards for protecting e-PHI. The best way to manage this and ensure that you are meeting all reasonable and appropriate controls is to use an information security program. An information security program is the highest level requirement. This wizard will guide you through the preprocess of building one for your practice.

/Physical Access Control

Dumpster diving is a common method attackers use gain any importation that could be used to aid in an attack. Recently this type of threat has become more important as our information systems often use removable media that can have data recovered even when the files are deleted.

/System Monitoring

Your information systems often generate logs that could indicate a breach or malware on your information systems. Identifying and correcting issues from this information can prevent you from having attackers steal your ePHI data.

/Data Continuity

Ransomware is an attack where attacker infect your computer systems with Malware that encrypts and prevents you from using your systems. The attackers often require a payment to unlock your data. However, the FBI has recommended that frequent backups and system restores are a faster and safer option than interacting with attackers.
